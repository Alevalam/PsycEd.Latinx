Addressing Mental Health

Stigma in Latinx Communities

A Parent Intervention

Program Overview


DURATION

  • 6 weeks of bi-weekly sessions
  • 4 hour sessions

LOVE

JOB DONE



PARTICIPANTS

  • Recruit participants at community centers like schools, churches, etc.
  • Latinx parents of middle schoolers
  • Pomona, CA

CERTIFIED

RESOURCES

  • Mental health tests
    •

OBJECTIVES


  1. Educate about mental health in general
  2. Educate about stigma and reduce participant stigma (Self Stigma and Perceived Stigma)
  3. Increase likelihood of people seeking resources and giving access to resources (Talk about self-reliance)

WHY THIS MATTERS


  • Middle school is a time when mental health issues start to come up
  • Lack of comprehensive culturally specific programs
  • Stigma towards mental health in the Latinx community

CULTURAL FACTORS TO CONSIDER


  • Importance of Respect
  • Religious Affiliations
  • Familismo
  • Machismo
  • Immigration
  • Language Barriers

BENEFITS OF INTEGRATED PSYCHOEDUCATION

  • Psychoeducational interventions are less expensive, more easily administered and potentially more accessible than conventional pharmacological and psychological interventions (Donker et al., 2009)
  • Education focused anti-stigma programs have repeatedly been found to be effective at reducing stigma related to mental illness, psychosis, and depression (Griffiths et al., 2014)
  • Latinos are now the largest minority group in the United States, comprising more than 35 million people or 12.5% of the total US population (Nadeem et al., 2007)
  • Briefness of the face-to-face Intervention component of the program is more feasible for participants who want to learn about mental health since the commitment is only three sessions and voluntary

RECRUITMENT AND INCENTIVES


  • Flyers in community centers, churches, schools
  • Incentives:
    • Childcare (for the duration of the session)
    • Breakfast and lunch: Ethnic foods from community vendors (30 minutes each)
    • Technology: Android tablet to be used throughout the sessions to access resources on website and will be kept by participants after completion of the program (One per family)

RESEARCH TEAM

  • Researchers from research group designing program
  • Bilingual staff to help with translations
  • Collaborate with therapists/counselors from nearby community mental health centers (Incentive being publicity of their services/potential increased use of their servives)

ASSESSMENT METHODS


Possible metrics for measuring participants’ stigma: (ideally 20-30 mins of testing since there is a pre (and post test?) and lunch takes up 30 mins) leaves 2 hours 30 mins for program approx.
  • Attitudes toward seeking professional psychological help scale
  • Self-stigma of seeking help scale
  • Internalized Stigma of Mental Illness Scale
  • Mental health recovery measure
  • Boston University empowerment scale
  • Multidimensional scale of perceived social support
  • Parental Attitudes Toward Psychological Services Inventory → to determine likelihood that parents seek mental health care for their children should they need.

CURRENT LIMITATIONS


  • Limited amount of time
  • Limited amount of space in the program
  • Limited amount of researchers available to speak with participants
  • Possible drop out: fear of stigmatization
  • Retention of information may be low due to the limited sessions or if resources are not used


DIRECTIONS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH AND PROGRAM EXPANSION


  • Other age groups: parents with younger/older children, program for children, program for teens, program for older adults
  • Ambassador program so that participants can generally educate others on mental health and receive a certification “Ally for Combatting Stigma against Mental Health”
  • Incorporate Guest Speakers who are famous in Latinx community into Program Intervention
  • Why? Referent Power→ People will be more influenced to seek treatment simply because of their liking/admirability of someone (French, Raven & Cartwright, 2016)
