Addressing Mental Health

Stigma in Latinx Communities

A Parent Intervention

Program Overview


DURATION

  • 3 sessions, bi-weekly
  • 4 hour sessions

BILINGUAL

  • Sessions in English and Spanish

MENTAL HEALTH

  • Each session focuses on a different aspect of mental health.



OBJECTIVES


  • Session 1: Introduction to mental health in general
  • Session 2: Education about the types of stigma and reduce participants stigma (Self Stigma, Perceived Stigma, + Self-Reliance)
  • Session 3: Increase likelihood of people seeking resources / giving access to resources (talk about self-reliance)

BENEFITS OF INTEGRATED PSYCHOEDUCATION

  • Education focused anti-stigma programs have repeatedly been found to be effective at reducing stigma related to mental illness, psychosis, and depression (Griffiths et al., 2014)
  • Research suggests that Psychoeducation effectively reduced stigma in Black patients who were unsure about treatment (Alvidrez et al., 2009)
  • Educating random Latinx community members through psychoeducation was associated with more positive attitudes toward seeking health (Dueweke & Bridges 2017)

PERCEIVED CHALLENGES FOR THE PROGRAM


  • Limited amount of time for participants
  • Limited amount of space in the program
  • Limited amount of researchers available to speak with participants
  • Possible drop out: fear of stigmatization
  • Retention of information may be low due to the limited sessions or if resources are not used


SOLUTIONS TO THESE CHALLENGES

  • Make the program more of an event to dedicate the day to
  • Hire additional outside assistance to run the program
  • Provide 24/7 accessible online resources from the program
  • Technology, Food, Child-care, Familismo incentives, Having confianza in the mind of researchers
  • Emphasize importance of knowing this information for family well-being

ASSESSMENT METHODS



  • Attitudes toward seeking professional psychological help scale
    • measure of mental health seeking attitudes
  • Internalized Stigma of Mental Illness Scale
    • designed to measure internalized stigma felt by those who have mental illnesses
  • Multidimensional scale of perceived social support
    • measures perceptions of support from family, friends, and partner
  • Parental Attitudes Toward Psychological Services Inventory
    • Attitudes towards mental health care
    • Likelihood that parents seek mental health care for their children should they need it

Why Mental Health Matters in Latinx Community

Lack of comprehensive culturally specific programs (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2001)



12.5% Total Population

Latinos are now the largest minority group in the United States(Nadeem et al., 2007)

Less utilization of services and Latinos are less likely to report mental health illness (McMiller & Weisz, 1996)

5.9% English-speaking Latinos reported receiving specialty care in the past year (Alegría et al., 2002)

PARTICIPANTS


  • 100 Latinx parents of middle schoolers
    • Indirectly addressing middle school child mental health stigma
      • By 8th grade over 30% of children had moderate-high levels of stigma (Chandra & Minkovitz, 2006)
  • Pomona, CA

WHAT IS MENTAL HEALTH

  • Definition: “Conditions that affect cognition, emotion, and behavior ”(Manderscheid et al., 2010)
    • Depression, anxiety, and other mental illnesses which have negative physiological effects
  • Mental Health can influence physiological health
    • Higher positive affect correlated with reduced health symptoms and increased longevity (Manderscheid et al., 2010)
    • Positive emotional style was related to better immune system functioning (Manderscheid et al., 2010)
    • Internalized stigma was related to worse mental and physical health outcomes (Pearl et al., 2017)


MACHISMO'S ROLE IN MENTAL HEALTH

  • Machismo - the positive and negative attitudes or beliefs about how to be a man (Nuñez et al., 2016)
  • Certain aspects of Machismo relate to negative cognitive behaviors (Nuñez et al., 2016)
    • Dominance, sexism, + emotional restrictiveness correlated with anxiety
    • Hostility and Mistrust
    • Caballerismo (Honor + Bravery) correlated with higher likelihood of angry reactions



INFLUENCE OF FAMILISMO ON MENTAL HEALTH

  • Strong familismo correlated with higher use of informal/religious care (Villatoro, Morales, & Mays, 2014)
    • Religion is important for dealing with mental illness as well as maintaining good mental health (McField & Belliard, 2009)
      • Latinos consider confessing as a form of therapy
    • Familismo did not serve as a barrier to mental health care use
      • Individuals in need of mental health care tended to have less family support
      • Family increased the likelihood of seeking mental health care if individuals did need treatment
    • Increased family conflict leads to worse mental health (Rivera et al., 2008)

STIGMA

  • Definition: A mark separating individuals from one another based on a socially conferred judgment that some persons or groups are tainted and “less than.” (Pescosolido, Lang, & Olafsdottir, 2008)
    • Stigma often leads to endorsement of stereotypes, prejudice, and discrimination (Vogel, Wade, & Haake, 2006)
  • Self-Stigma: the decrease in a person’s self-esteem due to that person viewing him or herself as socially unacceptable (Vogel, Wade & Haake, 2006)
  • Perceived-Stigma (Public-Stigma): The notion from people or society that a person is socially unacceptable (Vogel, Wade,& Haake, 2006)
  • Self-Reliance: the tendency for a person to feel that he or she can handle all of their problems alone (Jennings et al., 2015)

SELF-STIGMA, PERCEIVED-STIGMA, SELF-RELIANCE

  • Jennings et al., (2017) conducted research exploring the relationship between these 3 variables
    • Research found that increases in public-stigma correlated with increases in self-stigma
    • Individuals who had higher self-stigma tended to be more self-reliant
    • Increased self-reliance led to negative treatment-seeking attitudes and behaviors
    • Being overly self-reliant led to negative treatment-seeking attitudes and behaviors

WHY REDUCE STIGMA?

  • Self-stigma negatively influences the likelihood of seeking mental health care (Lannin et al., 2016)
  • Research illustrates that internalized stigma can negatively impact physical health in individuals (Pearl et al., 2017)
  • Experiences of rejection and discrimination as a result of public stigma decreases quality of life for those with mental illness and their families (Pescosolido, Lang, & Olafsdottir, 2008)
  • Stigma interferes with mental health by decreasing people’s self esteem and social opportunities (Corrigan, 2004)
  • Lower levels of stigma were associated with more positive attitudes in seeking help (Dueweke & Bridges, 2017 )

EXAMPLE OF VIGNETTE (ATAQUE DE NERVIOS)

Diana (42 years old) has not been feeling like herself lately. Her father passed away a week ago. Her family has noticed that he has been more aggressive. She’s almost a completely different person. She came into the house unusually late last night and when asked about her whereabouts by her family, she broke several things in her house. Her family believes she is experiencing “ataque de nervios” due to her father’s passing.

  • Culture-bound syndromes could be confused with other disorders
  • Ataque De Nervios ≠ Panic Disorder
  • Culture-bound syndromes have comorbidity with other disorders like anxiety and panic disorder.
    • Ataque de nervios is generally present in the Latinx population and they might believe that treatment is not “abnormal” or “uncommon”
  • Why is this a problem?

HOW DOES STIGMA REDUCTION BENEFIT MENTAL HEALTH?

  • Only 20% of Latinos with symptoms of a psychological disorder talk to a doctor about their concerns, and only 10 percent contact a mental health specialist (“National Alliance on Mental Illness”)
  • Not asking for help can make the issue more severe for your child
  • Think about the benefits of your child receiving support
  • Realize that Mental Illness is important
    • United States is investing more $ to address current disparities and helping people find appropriate resources
    • Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) has announced $195 million in funding opportunities for community health centers
    • There are many support centers already available, including us!

INCREASING TREATMENT SEEKING HABITS

  • Planning next steps after the end of the program
    • Inform them of constant access to online program resources
    • Encourage healthy social behavior by maintaining support systems
      • Relationships within the family (Villatoro, Morales, and Mays, 2014)
      • Relationships within the community
        • Stay in touch with people from the program
        • Build relationships with those in the community
        • Encourage religious behaviors (McField & Belliard, 2009)


HOW TO SUPPORT PEOPLE WITH MENTAL HEALTH

  • Learn how to be an ally for mental health
  • Create an action plan for “HOW” to support
    • Be AVAILABLE → Make sure your child knows you are here for them
    • Listen to them!
    • If this is a friend, ask them if there is anything that you can do for them
    • Reassure them that you care about them
    • Find other ways to educate yourself
      • Become an ambassador for teaching others about mental health
      • Do research
    • Help them find the appropriate treatment

DIRECTIONS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH AND PROGRAM EXPANSION


  • Other age groups: parents with younger/older children, program for children, program for teens, program for older adults
  • Ambassador program so that participants can generally educate others on mental health and receive a certification “Ally for Combatting Stigma against Mental Health”
  • Incorporate Guest Speakers who are famous in Latinx community into Program Intervention
    • Referent Power→ People will be more influenced to seek treatment simply because of their liking/admirability of someone (French, Raven & Cartwright, 2016)

PROGRAM DEDICATION TO COMMUNITY

  • Sending a thank you letter or telephone calls to participants and asking about their well-being
    • Creates a sense of family “familismo” with our participants
    • Fosters “confianza” or trust to reach out to the program mentors whenever
  • Providing participants with further information and access to additional resources through the website
  • Getting mental health professionals from the Pomona area
  • Partnering with local food vendors for breakfast and lunch
  • Hiring local child care professionals to take care of children during session
